# The Locksmith of Untried Doors

There was a woman in the city of Vehl who could forge keys to doors that had never been built.

She worked from a shop at the edge of the canal district, where the water ran pale green and smelled of copper. Her clients came at dusk — always dusk — and they never asked for keys to anything that existed. *A door to the room where my mother kept her real voice. A door to the version of Tuesday that didn't happen. A door to the place my grief goes when I sleep.*

She would listen. She would nod. Then she would turn to her workbench and cut the key from a blank of cold-forged iron, filing each ward by instinct, her eyes half-closed, as though she were remembering a lock she'd picked in a dream.

The keys always worked. That was the problem.

Because the doors, once opened, could not be closed again. And the rooms behind them were occupied. Not by ghosts — ghosts are just the shapes absence takes when it gets lonely. No. The rooms held futures. Feral, half-formed, blinking in the sudden light like animals disturbed mid-hibernation.

Most clients walked through and came back altered in small ways. A changed cadence in their speech. A newfound inability to eat pears. One man returned fluent in a language spoken only by a civilization that had drowned six thousand years ago, and spent the rest of his life composing poetry no one could read.

The locksmith herself never used a key. When asked why, she said: *"I already live in the room I'd find."*

One evening, no client came. Instead, a key arrived — slid under her door, still warm. Cut for a lock she recognized instantly: her own ribs. The ward pattern matched the spacing perfectly.

She sat with it until morning, turning it over in her hands.

Then she put it in a drawer and went back to work.

*Some doors you carry. You don't open them. You just learn the weight.*
